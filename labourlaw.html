What is Labour Law?

Labour Law Definition:


.

Labour law, also known as employment law or labor and employment law, is a legal framework that governs the rights, responsibilities, and relationships between employers, employees, and labor unions in the workplace. It covers a wide range of issues, including employment contracts, wages, working conditions, workplace safety, discrimination, and collective bargaining. The purpose of labour law is to protect the rights of workers and ensure fair and equitable treatment in the workplace. It varies from country to country and is often influenced by national legislation, international treaties, and labor standards.

Rules Of Labour Law:

Minimun Wage: Labor laws typically set a minimum wage that employers must pay to their employees.This wage is designed to ensure that workers receive a basic level of compensation for their work. Working Hours: Regulations usually limit the number of hours an employee can work in a day or week to prevent overwork. Overtime rules also govern compensation for work beyond standard hours. Anti-Discrimination: Labor laws prohibit discrimination in hiring, promotion, and termination based on factors such as race, gender, age, disability, religion. Health And Safety: Employers are generally obligated to provide a safe and healthy working environment for their employees.Regulations may require safety training and the provision of protective equipment. Equal Pay: Laws may require employers to pay equal wages for equal work, regardless of gender or other factors. Child Labour: Child Labour: Regulations set restrictions on the employment of minors, including minimum age requirements and working hours for young workers.

Advantages Of The Labour Laws:

  • Collective Bargaining.
  • Employment Contracts.
  • Equal Opportunity and Non-Discrimination.
